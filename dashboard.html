Key Insights & Findings

LDA Wins Despite Theory
Despite Box's M test suggesting QDA (p < 0.05, unequal covariance), LDA achieved 4.79% higher accuracy. This demonstrates that simpler models can outperform complex ones when dealing with imbalanced data.
Critical Blind Spot: 44% Missed Cases
The model only identifies 56.25% of diabetic cases (specificity). This means 44% of diabetic patients go undetected, which is unacceptable for clinical screening. The root cause is class imbalance (65% vs 35%).
Class Imbalance Impact
The 2:1 ratio (No Diabetes vs Diabetes) causes the model to favor the majority class. While sensitivity is excellent (86.67%), specificity suffers significantly, making the model unsuitable for clinical deployment without addressing this imbalance.
Strong Discriminative Ability
LDA achieved an AUC-ROC of 0.835, indicating excellent ability to distinguish between diabetic and non-diabetic patients. Both models significantly outperform random chance (AUC = 0.5).
Robustness Over Complexity
QDA's flexibility (allowing different covariance matrices) led to overfitting on the smaller class. LDA's simpler assumption (equal covariance) proved more robust, demonstrating that theoretical optimality doesn't always translate to real-world performance.
Path Forward
Future improvements should focus on SMOTE (Synthetic Minority Over-sampling), cost-sensitive learning to prioritize sensitivity, and ensemble methods combining LDA with Random Forest or SVM for enhanced performance.
